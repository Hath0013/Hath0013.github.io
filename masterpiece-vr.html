MasterpieceVR

User Experience & Interface | Feature Design

OVERVIEW

MasterpieceVR (MPVR) was seeking a solution to meet the needs of a simple and easy-to-use annotation system that utilized cutting-edge industry tools. Working with MasterpieceVR involved designing and developing an aesthetically-pleasing and highly functional way for users to annotate their internal and external 3D creations.

ROLE

Lead UX Designer

User Research, Interaction, Visual design, Prototyping & User Testing

Background

MasterpieceVR is a 3D sculpting and painting tool that allows users to build tangible objects, colourful environments, and high quality models. It also allows users to sculpt with friends in the same room in real time, or invite spectators to watch while the user creates. Learn more about MPVR here.

I led the design team at MPVR.

I joined MPVR as a new interaction design grad and lead a team of 2 UX designers and 3 game developers. At this time, MPVR was a fresh startup. I lead the UX and UI across every aspect of our annotation project was responsible for ensuring our we stayed on track with our timeline and met all of our technical and usability requirements.

I learned valuable skills while working with MPVR and thrived in the cutting-edge tech environment. During this time I was able to accomplish these key achievements:

  • Implemented a design process. This was key in helping our team establish more structure to how we conducted our work.
  • Ran a daily scrum meeting. As a brand new team of grads, we were lacking structure. I began running daily scrum meetings where we could each say what we'd done the day previous, what our plan was for the day ahead and any blocks preventing work from happening.

Understanding the problem

Being a new and niche tool, MPVR was working quickly as a startup to set itself apart from competitors. To open themselves up to a bigger market, they needed a tool that could be utilized by professors and students. Providing this annotation tool would enable professors and students to take and leave notes while in a virtual space.

My research encompassed:

  • Understanding what UX looks like in a virtual space
  • Discovering what current typing methods were available in VR
  • Learning what annotation methods were already available

Developing a process

We had a three month window to deliver our solution. To effectively kick off our project, we ran five day design sprint following Jake Knapp's process in The Sprint Book. Using this sprint method we could accelerate our approach towards innovating and solving our problem.

Day 1: Map

By mapping the user journey, we could better understand how many potential steps the user would need to take. By analyzing all the possible solutions, we could also see steps in the process that could be condensed or removed all together, simplifying the journey.

Day 2: Sketch

Next, we sketched potential solutions of what our solution could look like. Seeing everyone's thoughts on paper told us a few things: everyone had a consistency to their previously mapped user journey and everyone had a unique solution for our problem. A few team members shared the same ideas but everyone had a different way of putting those ideas into a conceptual sketch.

Day 3: Decision

We all had the chance to vote using coloured dot stickers to place on the elements of each map and sketch that we thought would drive the best solution. After careful review from the CEO of the company and our project lead, a map and a sketch were chosen. These would be our focus for the remainder of our timeline.

Day 4: Prototype

We worked closely with the game developers to create a rapid prototype. I would test the prototype in virtual reality as it was being created to ensure it was on target with the previously selected map. The visuals used in our prototype at this point were simple shapes such as blocks, spheres and cones.

Day 5: Test

We ran user testing on day 5 with a mixed pool of students and professors, some who were familiar with virtual reality and some who were not. We gained valuable feedback from these initial tests which would fuel the next changes to our user journey mapping and user interface design.

Meeting objectives

Running our sprint revealed key targets that our design solution needed to meet which I've listed below:

  • Invest the majority of our time collecting feedback from user testing and using that feedback to inform re-designing for the best possible user experience
  • Conduct extensive research on cutting edge annotation tools on the VR market
  • Structure interface design and user flow to facilitate the user’s behaviour
  • Ensure ease of use for all users independent of their level of virtual technology understanding
  • Provide a clear method of interface navigation
  • Add user engagement tools
  • Plan for additional future digital initiatives

Results and takeaways

We presented our final working prototype and ran a live demo to students and professors. We had a lot of great feedback and carried this forward with a final project report. I created some whiteboard illustrations to support the brand and visual display of our live demo.

Some key takeaways from this project:

  • Using a design sprint is powerful. Kicking off our project with this method helped our team focus on the problem and innovate to solve it.
  • Scrum goes a long way. Our team struggled with communication and working cohesively. Introducing a daily 15 minute scrum meeting made a world of difference.
  • User testing doesn't end after development. Design is a constant iteration of improving the experience for the end user. Always find ways to collect and listen to your user's feedback.
